Microsoft (MSFT) — The Operating System of the Future

Why it's in this issue: Position size now 11.1% of the ISA — that is 2.2× the 5% target weight. P&L is -20.0% vs entry, but the real Sell/Trim question is portfolio construction, not gains. Rebalancing inside the ISA is costless.

Apple captured the world's imagination. Microsoft quietly captured something even bigger — the world's infrastructure. Apple might power your pocket, but Microsoft powers your office, your bank, your school, your cloud, and sometimes even your Saturday night gaming. That's more than just staying relevant. That's lasting power.

Moat: Embedded in daily life

Microsoft has built itself into the foundation of modern life. Windows still runs on over a billion devices worldwide. The Office Suite — from Outlook to Excel to Teams — is the default language of business. Azure has become a heavyweight rival to Amazon Web Services, anchoring the next wave of digital infrastructure. This isn't a brand advantage like Apple's. It's the plumbing of the digital world. Removing it would mean rebuilding how everything works.

Financial strength

Microsoft doesn't just make money — it multiplies it. Revenue flows in from cloud, software licensing, gaming, LinkedIn and advertising. Software scales without big costs, giving the company enviable margins. And Microsoft is one of only two companies left in the world holding a AAA credit rating — that's more than a badge, it tells you investors trust Microsoft almost as much as they trust government bonds. Very few companies ever reach that level of security.

Innovation

For years Microsoft was mocked as slow and uninspired. Not anymore. The OpenAI partnership has supercharged Word, Excel and Teams with AI capabilities — productivity is being rewritten in real time. Azure is growing at pace, making Microsoft a critical player in the digital era. Xbox and Game Pass are building the Netflix of gaming. And strategic acquisitions — LinkedIn, Activision Blizzard — show a company that has mastered the art of buying into the future. This isn't the Microsoft of the early 2000s; it's leaner, hungrier, and far more visionary.

The next 100 years

Why does Microsoft belong in the 100-Year Survivor List? It's indispensable — governments, banks and schools run on it. It's adaptive — from PCs to cloud to AI, it has reinvented itself multiple times. It's trusted — few companies command this level of institutional confidence. In short, Microsoft isn't a luxury. It's infrastructure. And infrastructure, when combined with adaptability, has been built for centuries.

📘 Why pullbacks matter to long-term investors

A 15%+ pullback from a recent high is not a guarantee of a bottom — it's a reminder that the market re-prices quality on short-term fear. The long-term investor's edge is patience: a quality compounder bought 20% off its high tends to outperform the same business bought at the high, provided the underlying thesis (revenue growth, margins, moat) hasn't broken. Always pair price action with a one-line thesis check before adding.

📘 Why trimming winners is a long-term discipline

Letting winners run is good. Letting them run *unchecked* until they dominate your portfolio concentrates idiosyncratic risk in a way most long-term investors regret in the next 30% drawdown. In a tax-shielded wrapper (ISA/SIPP) trimming back to target weight costs nothing and preserves the compounding base. The rule isn't 'sell because it's up' — it's 'sell because the position size now distorts your plan'.

📘 Why thesis-break is the only sell signal that matters

Price tells you what the market thinks today; the thesis tells you what you believed when you bought. When the gap widens — deep drawdowns with no fundamental story, or quiet outperformance you can't explain — that's the moment to re-read the original case. Long-term investors don't react to the chart; they reassess whether the *business* still matches the story.

📘 Why one-day moves are noise, until they aren't

A 5%+ single-day move on 2× volume usually has a cause. Sometimes the cause is real (earnings, guidance, M&A); sometimes it's a sector rotation or a macro headline. The discipline: name the cause *before* acting, and only fold it into your thesis if it changes the long-term picture. Most movers are content fodder, not portfolio decisions.

A closing thought

The point of running this scan twice a week is not to make you act twice a week. It is to give you the smallest possible number of well-reasoned decisions, taken calmly, with your notes to yourself in front of you. Most weeks the honest answer will be to do nothing — which is exactly why the discipline is worth having.
